Legal Restrictions on Muslims

Rechtliche Beschränkungen für Muslime

After a 2003 Constitutional Court ruling, half of Germany’s states enacted legislation that restricted religious dress for teachers and other civil servants. This law was passed under a guise of feminism, neutrality in religion, and to promote the integration of Muslims in Germany. However, preceding these restrictions, parliamentary debates and documents “emphasized the need to recognize the … tradition shaped by Christianity (and Judaism).” Therefore, the restrictions clearly targeted Muslim women who wear a headscarf. This was a popular topic in the media and politics. In fact, politicians such as Gerhard Schröder and Renate Schmidt outwardly disapproved of the headscarf. Meanwhile, Marieluise Beck along with other prominent women voiced their opposition to the legislation. This aligns with the notion that “under colonial and secular modernity” it is often agreed that the veil is “treated with scorn and hostility.”

On an individual level, Muslim women often chose between removing their headscarf or losing their jobs. Collectively, this law alienated Muslim women and supported Islamophobia in Germany.
