Surveillance and Terrorism Related Restrictions

Überwachung und Terrorismus Einschränkungen

In its efforts to combat terrorism, the German government has emphasized the need for the protection of civil rights and liberties for both its citizens and non-citizens. However, some policies nonetheless targeted Muslims living in Germany and were criticized as racist and discriminatory.

The southern German state of Baden-Württemberg implemented a rule requiring applicants applying for German citizenship from certain Muslim-majority countries be sujected to tougher questioning, effective January 2006. The policy only applied to applicants from 57 Muslim-majority countries, and included questions on religious freedom, gender equality, homosexuality, freedom of expression, the concept of honor, and forced marriage. This policy has been widely criticized as discriminatory and racist. While the intention of the policy was to test the loyalty of applicants applying for German citizenship, it is hard to justify why only Muslims are subjected to enhanced questioning while it is not necessary for others to do so. The policy inherently discriminated against Muslims because it perceived them as potentially disloyal to Germany and therefore should be subjected to additional questioning. In addition, the contents of the new questions also drew debate on whether they really test loyalty to Germany or are just meant to instill fear in Muslim applicants. For instance, a portion of the German public do not support LGBTQ rights so it is unfair to question citizenship applicants about their views on this issue.

In April 2017, German lawmakers approved a partial ban on burqas, prohibiting public servants - including election officials, military, and judicial staff - from wearing the burqa when working. Far-right politicians in Germany even called for a total ban on burqas, though this effort did not succeed. The policy was implemented in the aftermath of several terrorist attacks in Germany, including one where a truck killed 12 people at a Berlin Christmas market. It was also implemented ahead of the German federal elections in September 2017. This policy has been criticized for discriminating against Muslim women in Germany. While the policy was aimed at combating terrorism, it targeted Muslim women who wear the burqa and their religious rights were infringed.

Lastly, German police has been accused of racial profiling and race-based surveillance practices. German police were criticised for internally using the term Nafris, which is a term for North African repeated offenders. In practice, the term was used to identify and surveil North Africans in general. For instance, police officers questioned hundreds of North African men in Cologne on 2017’s New Year’s Eve simply because of their race and referred to the men as Nafris in a tweet. The police justified their actions as necessary to combat crime, after hundreds of reports of sexual assaults on New Year’s Eve a year prior. Some criticized the police’s actions as race profiling because the men were targeted due to their national origin. This is clearly an instance of guilt by association, where the men were targeted preemptively for being of North African origin and being out to celebrate New Year’s Eve in Cologne.
